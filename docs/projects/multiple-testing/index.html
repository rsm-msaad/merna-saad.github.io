Going deeper

Every practicing analyst has been here. You run an A/B test. It comes back significant. You celebrate. Then someone asks, “But did you correct for the fact that you tested twelve metrics?” and the room goes quiet.

Multiple testing is one of those topics that separates careful analysis from accidental storytelling. It shows up everywhere: clinical trials comparing a drug across dozens of endpoints, marketing teams testing button colors and headline variants and pricing tiers all at once, genomics researchers scanning twenty thousand genes for differential expression. The math does not care about the domain. If you run enough tests, you will find something. The question is whether what you found is real.

The setup: why one test at five percent feels safe

A p-value is, at its core, a measure of surprise. You start with a boring assumption (the null hypothesis) and ask: “If this boring assumption were true, how likely is it that I would see data at least this extreme?” When the answer is “very unlikely,” say less than 5%, you reject the boring assumption and declare the result significant.

Take a coin. You flip it 100 times and get 47 heads. Is it fair? The null hypothesis says yes, \(p = 0.5\). You compute a p-value: the probability of seeing 47 or fewer heads (or 53 or more, since the test is two-sided) if the coin really is fair. That probability turns out to be quite high. You cannot reject the null. The coin passes the test.

The 5% threshold (\(\alpha = 0.05\)) means you are willing to be wrong 5% of the time. Specifically, if the null hypothesis is true (the coin really is fair), there is a 5% chance your test will incorrectly declare it unfair. This is a Type I error, a false positive. One in twenty. For a single test, that feels like a reasonable trade.

But “one in twenty” is not zero. And when you start running more than one test, those small chances start stacking up.

The trap: what happens with one hundred tests

Imagine you have 100 perfectly fair coins. You test each one individually at the 5% level. Each test has a 5% chance of a false positive. The probability that none of them produce a false positive is \((1 - 0.05)^{100} = 0.0059\). Flip that around: the probability that at least one test gives a false positive is \(1 - (1 - 0.05)^{100} \approx 0.994\). Ninety-nine point four percent.

You are virtually guaranteed to “discover” something, even though nothing is there.

This is the family-wise error rate (FWER): the probability of making at least one false positive across all your tests. With \(m\) tests at significance level \(\alpha\), the FWER is \(1 - (1 - \alpha)^m\). It grows fast. At 10 tests, you are at 40%. At 20 tests, 64%. At 50 tests, 92%. The curve is not linear; it is exponential in its approach to certainty.

The simulation below runs this experiment 1,000 times. Each time, we test 100 fair coins and count how many produce a false positive.

Show the simulation code 
from scipy.stats import binom

np.random.seed(42)
n_sims, n_tests, n_flips = 1000, 100, 100

all_heads = np.random.binomial(n_flips, 0.5, (n_sims, n_tests))
left_tail = binom.cdf(all_heads, n_flips, 0.5)
right_tail = 1 - binom.cdf(all_heads - 1, n_flips, 0.5)
p_values_sim = 2 * np.minimum(left_tail, right_tail)

fp_counts = (p_values_sim < 0.05).sum(axis=1)
print(f"Average false positives per 100 fair-coin tests: {fp_counts.mean():.2f}")
print(f"Simulations with at least one false positive: {(fp_counts > 0).mean() * 100:.1f}%")
Average false positives per 100 fair-coin tests: 3.55
Simulations with at least one false positive: 96.9%

Bonferroni: the conservative answer

The Bonferroni correction is the oldest and simplest fix. Instead of testing each hypothesis at \(\alpha = 0.05\), you test at \(\alpha / m\), where \(m\) is the number of tests. With 100 tests, the new threshold becomes \(0.05 / 100 = 0.0005\). Each test must clear a much higher bar.

The intuition is that you are spreading your “5% error budget” equally across all tests. If each test uses only \(0.05/m\) of the budget, the total probability of any false positive stays below \(\alpha\). This guarantees FWER control.

The cost is power. Bonferroni makes it very hard to detect real effects, especially when they are subtle. In the theater animation above, we saw 20 true effects but Bonferroni only caught 3 of them. The threshold was so strict that most real signals could not clear it. When you are exploring a large space and you expect many real effects, Bonferroni throws away too much truth.

Show the Bonferroni implementation 
np.random.seed(42)
heads_single = np.random.binomial(100, 0.5, size=100)
left_single = binom.cdf(heads_single, 100, 0.5)
right_single = 1 - binom.cdf(heads_single - 1, 100, 0.5)
p_values = 2 * np.minimum(left_single, right_single)

alpha = 0.05
m = 100
bonferroni_threshold = alpha / m
significant = sum(1 for p in p_values if p < bonferroni_threshold)
print(f"Bonferroni threshold: {bonferroni_threshold}")
print(f"Significant after Bonferroni: {significant} out of {m}")
Bonferroni threshold: 0.0005
Significant after Bonferroni: 0 out of 100

Benjamini-Hochberg: a smarter line

The key insight behind Benjamini-Hochberg (1995) is that sometimes you do not need to prevent every false positive. You just need to control the proportion of your discoveries that are false. This is the false discovery rate (FDR): among all the hypotheses you reject, what fraction are actually null?

FWER asks: “Did I make any mistakes at all?” FDR asks: “What percentage of my discoveries are mistakes?” When you are screening thousands of genes or running hundreds of marketing experiments, the second question is often more practical. You expect some false positives. You just want to keep the rate manageable.

The BH procedure works in four steps. First, sort all \(m\) p-values from smallest to largest: \(p_{(1)} \leq p_{(2)} \leq \dots \leq p_{(m)}\). Second, for each p-value at rank \(k\), compute its threshold: \(k \cdot \alpha / m\). Third, find the largest rank \(k\) where the p-value is still below its threshold: \(p_{(k)} \leq k \cdot \alpha / m\). Fourth, reject all hypotheses at ranks 1 through \(k\).

The threshold line rises linearly from left to right. Early p-values must beat a strict bar. Later p-values get more room. This rising threshold is what gives BH its power: it adapts to the density of signals in your data instead of applying a single fixed cutoff.

Show the BH implementation 
def benjamini_hochberg(p_vals, alpha=0.05):
    p_array = np.array(p_vals)
    m = len(p_array)
    sorted_indices = np.argsort(p_array)
    sorted_p = p_array[sorted_indices]
    thresholds = np.arange(1, m + 1) * alpha / m
    below = sorted_p <= thresholds
    if not below.any():
        return []
    k = np.where(below)[0].max()
    return list(sorted_indices[:k + 1])

rejected = benjamini_hochberg(p_values)
print(f"BH rejected: {len(rejected)} out of {m}")
print(f"Bonferroni rejected: {significant} out of {m}")
BH rejected: 0 out of 100
Bonferroni rejected: 0 out of 100

When to use which

Use Bonferroni when each false alarm is catastrophic. In a clinical trial, incorrectly declaring that a drug works can lead to patients receiving ineffective treatment. In regulatory submissions, a single false positive can cost millions and erode public trust. In these settings, you want the strongest possible guarantee: no false positives, period. Bonferroni delivers that at the cost of missing some real effects.

Use Benjamini-Hochberg when you are exploring. Gene expression studies routinely test 20,000 hypotheses simultaneously. Marketing platforms run thousands of A/B tests per quarter. In these settings, a handful of false positives mixed into your discoveries is acceptable, as long as the rate stays controlled. BH gives you far more power to detect real signals while keeping the false discovery rate at your chosen level (typically 5%).

The choice is fundamentally about which mistake you can live with. Bonferroni minimizes the chance of being fooled at all. BH minimizes the chance of missing something real. Neither is universally correct. The right answer depends on the cost of each type of error in your specific context.

A closing reflection

Statistics is full of tools that promise certainty: confidence intervals, significance thresholds, rejection rules. But the whole field of multiple testing correction exists because those tools were never as certain as they seemed. Run enough tests and the guarantees break down. The corrections do not add certainty. They add honesty.

That honesty, the willingness to ask “how many of my discoveries might be wrong?”, is what separates careful analysis from noise dressed up as insight. Bonferroni and Benjamini-Hochberg are just two different ways of answering the same question. The important thing is that you ask it at all.

## Going deeper

Every practicing analyst has been here. You run an A/B test. It comes back significant. You celebrate. Then someone asks, "But did you correct for the fact that you tested twelve metrics?" and the room goes quiet.

Multiple testing is one of those topics that separates careful analysis from accidental storytelling. It shows up everywhere: clinical trials comparing a drug across dozens of endpoints, marketing teams testing button colors and headline variants and pricing tiers all at once, genomics researchers scanning twenty thousand genes for differential expression. The math does not care about the domain. If you run enough tests, you will find something. The question is whether what you found is real.

### The setup: why one test at five percent feels safe

A p-value is, at its core, a measure of surprise. You start with a boring assumption (the null hypothesis) and ask: "If this boring assumption were true, how likely is it that I would see data at least this extreme?" When the answer is "very unlikely," say less than 5%, you reject the boring assumption and declare the result significant.

Take a coin. You flip it 100 times and get 47 heads. Is it fair? The null hypothesis says yes, $p = 0.5$. You compute a p-value: the probability of seeing 47 or fewer heads (or 53 or more, since the test is two-sided) if the coin really is fair. That probability turns out to be quite high. You cannot reject the null. The coin passes the test.

The 5% threshold ($\alpha = 0.05$) means you are willing to be wrong 5% of the time. Specifically, if the null hypothesis is true (the coin really is fair), there is a 5% chance your test will incorrectly declare it unfair. This is a Type I error, a false positive. One in twenty. For a single test, that feels like a reasonable trade.

But "one in twenty" is not zero. And when you start running more than one test, those small chances start stacking up.

```{python}
#| code-summary: "Show the simulation code"
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(42)
flips = np.random.binomial(1, 0.5, size=100)
heads = flips.sum()
result = stats.binomtest(heads, 100, 0.5, alternative='two-sided')
print(f"Heads: {heads}, p-value: {result.pvalue:.4f}")
```

### The trap: what happens with one hundred tests

Imagine you have 100 perfectly fair coins. You test each one individually at the 5% level. Each test has a 5% chance of a false positive. The probability that *none* of them produce a false positive is $(1 - 0.05)^{100} = 0.0059$. Flip that around: the probability that *at least one* test gives a false positive is $1 - (1 - 0.05)^{100} \approx 0.994$. Ninety-nine point four percent.

You are virtually guaranteed to "discover" something, even though nothing is there.

This is the family-wise error rate (FWER): the probability of making at least one false positive across all your tests. With $m$ tests at significance level $\alpha$, the FWER is $1 - (1 - \alpha)^m$. It grows fast. At 10 tests, you are at 40%. At 20 tests, 64%. At 50 tests, 92%. The curve is not linear; it is exponential in its approach to certainty.

The simulation below runs this experiment 1,000 times. Each time, we test 100 fair coins and count how many produce a false positive.

```{python}
#| code-summary: "Show the simulation code"
from scipy.stats import binom

np.random.seed(42)
n_sims, n_tests, n_flips = 1000, 100, 100

all_heads = np.random.binomial(n_flips, 0.5, (n_sims, n_tests))
left_tail = binom.cdf(all_heads, n_flips, 0.5)
right_tail = 1 - binom.cdf(all_heads - 1, n_flips, 0.5)
p_values_sim = 2 * np.minimum(left_tail, right_tail)

fp_counts = (p_values_sim < 0.05).sum(axis=1)
print(f"Average false positives per 100 fair-coin tests: {fp_counts.mean():.2f}")
print(f"Simulations with at least one false positive: {(fp_counts > 0).mean() * 100:.1f}%")
```

### Bonferroni: the conservative answer

The Bonferroni correction is the oldest and simplest fix. Instead of testing each hypothesis at $\alpha = 0.05$, you test at $\alpha / m$, where $m$ is the number of tests. With 100 tests, the new threshold becomes $0.05 / 100 = 0.0005$. Each test must clear a much higher bar.

The intuition is that you are spreading your "5% error budget" equally across all tests. If each test uses only $0.05/m$ of the budget, the total probability of any false positive stays below $\alpha$. This guarantees FWER control.

The cost is power. Bonferroni makes it very hard to detect real effects, especially when they are subtle. In the theater animation above, we saw 20 true effects but Bonferroni only caught 3 of them. The threshold was so strict that most real signals could not clear it. When you are exploring a large space and you expect many real effects, Bonferroni throws away too much truth.

```{python}
#| code-summary: "Show the Bonferroni implementation"
np.random.seed(42)
heads_single = np.random.binomial(100, 0.5, size=100)
left_single = binom.cdf(heads_single, 100, 0.5)
right_single = 1 - binom.cdf(heads_single - 1, 100, 0.5)
p_values = 2 * np.minimum(left_single, right_single)

alpha = 0.05
m = 100
bonferroni_threshold = alpha / m
significant = sum(1 for p in p_values if p < bonferroni_threshold)
print(f"Bonferroni threshold: {bonferroni_threshold}")
print(f"Significant after Bonferroni: {significant} out of {m}")
```

### Benjamini-Hochberg: a smarter line

The key insight behind Benjamini-Hochberg (1995) is that sometimes you do not need to prevent every false positive. You just need to control the proportion of your discoveries that are false. This is the false discovery rate (FDR): among all the hypotheses you reject, what fraction are actually null?

FWER asks: "Did I make any mistakes at all?" FDR asks: "What percentage of my discoveries are mistakes?" When you are screening thousands of genes or running hundreds of marketing experiments, the second question is often more practical. You expect some false positives. You just want to keep the rate manageable.

The BH procedure works in four steps. First, sort all $m$ p-values from smallest to largest: $p_{(1)} \leq p_{(2)} \leq \dots \leq p_{(m)}$. Second, for each p-value at rank $k$, compute its threshold: $k \cdot \alpha / m$. Third, find the largest rank $k$ where the p-value is still below its threshold: $p_{(k)} \leq k \cdot \alpha / m$. Fourth, reject all hypotheses at ranks 1 through $k$.

The threshold line rises linearly from left to right. Early p-values must beat a strict bar. Later p-values get more room. This rising threshold is what gives BH its power: it adapts to the density of signals in your data instead of applying a single fixed cutoff.

```{python}
#| code-summary: "Show the BH implementation"
def benjamini_hochberg(p_vals, alpha=0.05):
    p_array = np.array(p_vals)
    m = len(p_array)
    sorted_indices = np.argsort(p_array)
    sorted_p = p_array[sorted_indices]
    thresholds = np.arange(1, m + 1) * alpha / m
    below = sorted_p <= thresholds
    if not below.any():
        return []
    k = np.where(below)[0].max()
    return list(sorted_indices[:k + 1])

rejected = benjamini_hochberg(p_values)
print(f"BH rejected: {len(rejected)} out of {m}")
print(f"Bonferroni rejected: {significant} out of {m}")
```

### When to use which

Use Bonferroni when each false alarm is catastrophic. In a clinical trial, incorrectly declaring that a drug works can lead to patients receiving ineffective treatment. In regulatory submissions, a single false positive can cost millions and erode public trust. In these settings, you want the strongest possible guarantee: no false positives, period. Bonferroni delivers that at the cost of missing some real effects.

Use Benjamini-Hochberg when you are exploring. Gene expression studies routinely test 20,000 hypotheses simultaneously. Marketing platforms run thousands of A/B tests per quarter. In these settings, a handful of false positives mixed into your discoveries is acceptable, as long as the rate stays controlled. BH gives you far more power to detect real signals while keeping the false discovery rate at your chosen level (typically 5%).

The choice is fundamentally about which mistake you can live with. Bonferroni minimizes the chance of being fooled at all. BH minimizes the chance of missing something real. Neither is universally correct. The right answer depends on the cost of each type of error in your specific context.

### A closing reflection

Statistics is full of tools that promise certainty: confidence intervals, significance thresholds, rejection rules. But the whole field of multiple testing correction exists because those tools were never as certain as they seemed. Run enough tests and the guarantees break down. The corrections do not add certainty. They add honesty.

That honesty, the willingness to ask "how many of my discoveries might be wrong?", is what separates careful analysis from noise dressed up as insight. Bonferroni and Benjamini-Hochberg are just two different ways of answering the same question. The important thing is that you ask it at all.
